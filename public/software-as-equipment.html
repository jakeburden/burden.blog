software as equipment

October 2018 Â· 4 minute read

I found myself making connections to how Heidegger’s analysis of equipment fits in the software world.

equipment is inconspicuous, unobtrusive, and non-obstinate

Using a hammer.

Driving a car.

Drinking coffee from a mug.

We don’t need to carefully consider each movement of our arms. It tends to feel like a natural process, like moving across a room.

Even as I’m typing this, I’m not pestering over each keystroke. I hardly think about my laptop as an object that is in front of me. However, if a key were to become stuck as a typed, then my laptop would become obvious to me.

When tools work, they evolve from being objects to being equipment. When equipment breaks down, becomes inert, or becomes non-functional, it starts devolving down to being just an object again.

software evolves into being equipment

Software is interesting, in that it’s not an object, yet it still becomes equipment. Our focus on software fades as we become accustomed to it’s interface. If the software is good, we tend to direct our focus on the task itself. Of course, software can become obtrusive. If a request fails, a bug becomes apparent, it’s slow to perform an operation, our focus shifts from the task we are set to work on, and back onto the software itself.

I’m unsure of what software metaphysically devolves into when it fails to be inconspicuous, unobtrusive, and non-obstinate. I don’t think Heidegger was interested in the metaphysical aspect of object into equipment, but more on the authenticity of the equipment. Perhaps it’s not that software simply becomes inauthentic when it breaks down, but that it is the inauthentic software that has a tendency to break down.

mass production of software

From Heidegger’s, History of the Concept of Time:

In simple craft conditions, every single work even has within it a reference to the particular prospective wearer and user. The work is as it were tailored to his body. Some goods are still custom-made and not mass-produced for the average “cross-section.” Mass-produced articles themselves do not lack reference, but it is quite generic; they have an indeterminacy, an arbitrariness, but they nevertheless have a reference to indeterminate others. What is of primary importance here is not the variety of modifications in content which arise, but the already given context of being from which they arise and which characterizes the presence of the work to be produced in concern.

My particular interest here is that software is very easily mass produced. It can be copied and distributed to such a high degree that we might was well consider it infinite.

How then, do we prevent software from becoming generic, arbitrary, and lacking reference? Is that even a goal that we have with software, or are we aiming for accessibility by producing for the average cross-section? We bloat websites with analytics to see if our “user flows” are matching expectation and validate our decisions. I’m not certain that this alone tells the whole story. Perhaps talking with the user, and having a conversation, would be more enlightening to their experience.

The industry seems to default to making mass produced software. Subscription models where every one gets the same thing, with perhaps some personalization after collecting data. There is nothing bad about this of course, and Heidegger isn’t suggesting that mass-produced equipment is bad either. Mass-production makes tools widely accessible.

I’m curious though, of what custom-made, hand-crafted software would offer.

What would be your version of movie streaming, social networking, book reading, messaging? If you make software, do you always pull from the off-the-shelf-tools? What would be your version of a framework, a database, or a UI library?

Standardization, consensus, protocol, and “battle tested”-ness, are indubitably useful for progress. I argue though, that we lean to far into favoring those attributes, and do not spend enough time exploring the other side. Building custom-made, non-standard, experimental software. Software that has a specific task is mind for a specific person. It might be just for you, or your friend, or your client. You might find that other people also like the theme of the software you’ve created.

The humanist aspect to software as equipment is that it is inconspicuous, unobtrusive, and non-obstinate for someone.
